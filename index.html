Meet Angelita: the little souvenir shop with one-of-a-kind Belizean treasures

★★★★★ 5.0 / 5 · A must-visit stop on Seaweed Street

If you buy one souvenir in Belize, buy it from Angelita. Tucked away on Seaweed Street in San Pedro, her little shop is the kind of place you almost walk right past, and the kind of place you tell every single friend about once you find it. This isn't a rack of mass-produced magnets shipped in by the container. Every piece on Angelita's shelves is genuinely handmade, and no two are ever alike. You are not buying a keepsake here. You are taking home a tiny, unrepeatable piece of Belize.

Angelita herself is the reason this spot earns a full five stars and then some. Warm, funny and endlessly generous with her time, she greets you like family the moment you step in. She'll walk you through where each carving, woven bag, beaded piece and hand-painted treasure came from, who made it, and what it means. Ask her about anything and she lights up. By the time you leave, you don't just have a souvenir, you have a story and a new friend on the island.

What makes it truly special is the handmade, one-of-a-kind promise. Because every item is crafted by local Belizean artisans, you'll never spot the exact same thing twice, not on her shelves and not in anyone else's suitcase back home. The wood carvings, the jewelry, the little painted pieces, they all carry the fingerprints of the person who made them. In a world of identical airport gift shops, that authenticity is rare, and it's exactly why travelers keep sending their friends straight to her door.

The prices are fair, the quality is real, and the experience is pure island magic. Whether you're hunting for the perfect gift, a meaningful memento of your trip, or just an excuse to chat with one of the friendliest locals in San Pedro, this is the stop to make. Do yourself a favor: skip the tourist-trap shops, wander down Seaweed Street, and ask for Angelita. You'll leave with something beautiful, something no one else has, and a smile that lasts the whole flight home.

Why Belize

A tiny country with a huge list of things to do

Belize is small. You can drive across most of it in a day. Still, it packs in more adventure than places ten times its size. On one trip you can snorkel a world-class reef, climb a Maya temple, and float through a jungle cave. Few places make that so easy.

Here is what makes it special. English is the official language, so there is no language barrier. The Belize dollar is pegged two to one with the US dollar, and US cash works almost everywhere. Flights from Texas or Florida take under four hours. As a result, Belize feels both exotic and simple at the same time.

The country splits neatly into two worlds. Inland you get jungle, rivers, caves and ancient ruins around San Ignacio and the Cayo District. Offshore you get the famous cayes, where life slows down and the reef does the talking. Most visitors mix both. In fact, that "surf and turf" combo is the classic Belize trip.

This guide walks you through the very best things to do in Belize for 2026. We cover the headline attractions, the quieter hidden gems, and the practical tips that save you money and time. Then we hand you a free drag-and-drop planner so you can build your own day-by-day list. Let's dive in.

Where To Go

Belize by region: pick your base

You don't need to see everything. Most great Belize trips pick one jungle base and one island base. Here's how the main areas stack up, plus the top things to do in each.

San Pedro & Ambergris Caye

The biggest island and the busiest reef scene. Base here for Hol Chan, Shark Ray Alley, Secret Beach golf-cart days, and lively bars. Great for first-timers who want amenities plus easy snorkeling.

Caye Caulker

"Go Slow" island life with no cars, just bikes and golf carts. Cheaper than San Pedro and full of charm. Swim at The Split, snorkel the reef, and watch the sunset with a rum punch.

San Ignacio & Cayo District

Belize's adventure capital. This is your base for the ATM Cave, Xunantunich, Caracol, cave tubing, zip lining and jungle lodges. Rent a 4x4 and chase waterfalls and ruins.

Placencia & the South

Sixteen miles of golden sand, a slow peninsula vibe, and gateway to whale sharks, Garifuna culture in Hopkins, and the Cockscomb jaguar reserve. Best for couples and beach lovers.

Short on time? Pair San Ignacio with Caye Caulker for the classic jungle-plus-island combo. Got two weeks? Add Placencia and the Hummingbird Highway. No matter how you split it, the planner below helps you keep it all straight.

The Complete Guide

Things to do in Belize, explained simply

Everything below is here to help you choose well and book smart. Read top to bottom, or jump to what matters for your trip.

Snorkeling and diving: the main event

Let's start with the obvious. The Belize Barrier Reef is the reason most people come, and it lives up to the hype. It stretches along nearly the whole coast and ranks as the largest reef in the Northern Hemisphere. Because it sits close to shore, you don't need to be an expert to enjoy it. In fact, you can wade out near Caye Caulker or San Pedro and see fish within minutes.

For first-timers, Hol Chan Marine Reserve is the classic choice. It's a protected cut in the reef where the water teems with rays, turtles, groupers and the odd reef shark. Right beside it sits Shark Ray Alley, where nurse sharks and stingrays gather in shallow water. Guides know exactly where to take you, so even nervous swimmers leave grinning. Meanwhile, serious divers chase the Great Blue Hole, a perfectly round sinkhole that drops more than 400 feet. It's a bucket-list dive, though the scenic flight over it is just as jaw-dropping.

If you only do one thing in Belize, make it a reef trip. Book a morning slot for calmer water and better light. Bring reef-safe sunscreen, since the strong sun reflects off the water. And go with a licensed operator who respects the reef, because protecting it keeps the magic alive.

Maya ruins and ancient history

Belize was once at the heart of the Maya world, and the ruins prove it. There are dozens of sites, and many sit half-swallowed by jungle, which makes exploring them feel like a discovery. Best of all, they're rarely crowded compared to ruins in Mexico or Guatemala.

Xunantunich is the easiest to reach from San Ignacio. You cross the river on a tiny hand-cranked ferry, then climb the El Castillo temple for views that stretch into Guatemala. Caracol is bigger and wilder, hidden deep in the Chiquibul Forest, and its main pyramid is still the tallest building in Belize. Lamanai is special too, since you reach it by riverboat, spotting crocodiles and birds along the way. Each site tells a different chapter, so even history skeptics tend to come away impressed.

Caves and jungle adventures

Inland Belize is all about the jungle, and the Cayo District is its beating heart. The headline act is the ATM Cave, short for Actun Tunichil Muknal. You hike through rainforest, swim across rivers, and squeeze into a sacred Maya cave where ancient pottery and crystallized skeletons still rest where they were left. It's strenuous and unforgettable, and cameras aren't allowed, so you experience it fully.

For an easier thrill, cave tubing is hard to beat. You float on an inner tube through cool, dark caverns, often capping the day with a zip line through the canopy. Families love it, since it's gentle but still feels like an adventure. Beyond that, you can rappel down waterfalls at Bocawina, hike to jaguar territory in the Cockscomb Basin, or join a guided night walk to meet creatures that only stir after dark.

Island life and beaches

When you're ready to slow down, head to the cayes. Ambergris Caye and its town of San Pedro offer the most to do, from reef trips to beach bars to a famous golf-cart ride out to Secret Beach. It's lively, social and easy. Caye Caulker, just south, is its smaller, cheaper sister. There are no cars, the motto is literally "Go Slow," and a swim at The Split followed by fresh lobster is a perfect afternoon.

On the mainland, Placencia delivers sixteen miles of golden sand and a relaxed peninsula vibe. It's also the launch point for whale shark season and Garifuna culture in nearby Hopkins. Wherever you land, the islands are where Belize trips wind down and the memories settle in.

Food, culture and the little moments

Belize punches above its weight on food and culture too. The country blends Creole, Garifuna, Mestizo, Maya and more, all speaking English with a warm Caribbean lilt. Start your day with fry jacks, fluffy fried dough you stuff with eggs or beans. For lunch, order rice and beans with stew chicken, a national staple. Down south, try hudut, a Garifuna fish stew rich with coconut and mashed plantain. Wash it all down with a cold Belikin, the local beer.

Don't rush past the small things either. Watching howler monkeys at dawn, sipping coconut water on a dock, or chatting with a guide about island life often becomes the trip's best memory. Belize rewards travelers who slow down and pay attention, which is exactly what the locals will tell you to do.

Sample itineraries to copy

Not sure how to fit it together? Here are three easy frameworks. For a 5-day trip, spend two nights in San Ignacio for ruins and caves, then three nights on Caye Caulker for reef and rest. For a 7-day trip, add a day for a Blue Hole or whale shark excursion, plus an extra island day. For 10 to 14 days, work in Placencia and the Hummingbird Highway, then end with slow island time. Use the planner above to drop these activities into your own day-by-day list.

However you build it, balance is the secret. Alternate a big adventure day with an easy beach day, so you don't burn out. Belize is small enough that you can do a lot, yet relaxed enough that you shouldn't try to do everything. Leave a little room to wander, and the country will fill it.

Getting around without stress

Belize is compact, so moving around is simpler than you'd think. Domestic flights link Belize City, San Pedro, Caye Caulker and Placencia in minutes, which is worth it when time is tight. Water taxis run regularly between the city and the islands. Inland, many travelers rent a 4x4 to chase ruins and waterfalls at their own pace, while local buses offer a cheap, colorful way to see daily life. On the islands themselves, you'll mostly walk, bike, or zip around in a golf cart.

One tip that saves headaches: build a little buffer into travel days, especially in green season when weather can delay boats. Keep your plans flexible, and a hiccup becomes a non-issue. That relaxed mindset is the whole point of a Belize vacation anyway.

Hidden gems and off-the-beaten-path picks

The famous spots earn their fame, but Belize hides quieter treasures too. If you want fewer crowds, these are worth seeking out. Crooked Tree Wildlife Sanctuary is a birder's paradise, home to thousands of native and migratory birds across a network of lagoons. The Community Baboon Sanctuary protects black howler monkeys, and you can often see them up close on a short guided walk. Both feel a world away from the busy cayes.

For nature lovers, the Mountain Pine Ridge Forest Reserve trades palm trees for cool pine forest, waterfalls and the chance to spot wildcats. The Maya King and Big Rock waterfalls reward a short hike with refreshing jungle pools. Out at sea, Tobacco Caye and the remote southern atolls offer castaway-style snorkeling with barely another soul around. Add one of these to your plan, and you'll come home with stories most visitors never get.

Even on the popular islands, you can find quiet. Rent a bike on Caye Caulker and pedal to the less-visited north side. Walk the back streets of San Pedro for local food stalls. The point is simple: a little curiosity goes a long way in Belize, and the country rewards it generously.

Belize for every kind of traveler

One reason Belize works so well is that it bends to fit your style. Families love it because English is spoken, the activities are gentle, and the Belize Zoo, cave tubing and shallow snorkeling all suit kids. Many parents say it's an easier tropical trip than Hawaii, with a short flight from the southern US and no currency headaches.

Couples find easy romance in sunset sails, jungle lodges and quiet beaches in Placencia. Adventure seekers get caves, reefs, zip lines and ruins all within a few hours of each other. Budget travelers stretch their money on Caye Caulker, local buses and street food, while luxury travelers have boutique jungle lodges and island resorts to choose from. Whoever you are, there's a version of Belize built for you.

How Belize compares to nearby destinations

People often weigh Belize against Costa Rica, Mexico or Hawaii, so here's the honest take. Compared to Costa Rica, Belize is smaller and easier to cover, with a stronger reef and the bonus of Maya ruins, though Costa Rica edges ahead on volcanoes and dense wildlife. Compared to Mexico's Riviera Maya, Belize is less developed and far less crowded, which is the whole appeal for many visitors. And compared to Hawaii, Belize is more affordable and quicker to reach from much of the US, while still offering that warm, English-speaking, easy-travel feel.

The bottom line is that Belize gives you a remarkable mix in one compact, friendly package. You get a top reef, real adventure, deep history and laid-back islands without the polish, the crowds or the price tag of the big-name destinations. For a lot of travelers, that's exactly the trade they're looking for.

Final advice before you book

Here's the short version of everything above. Pick one jungle base and one island base, then build your days around a few must-do experiences and plenty of downtime. Book the big-ticket tours ahead in busy season, and stay flexible in green season. Eat local, support local guides, and protect the reef. Most of all, lean into the "Go Slow" mindset, because the people who rush through Belize miss its best moments. Use the planner above, lock in your favorites, and get ready for one of the easiest great trips you'll ever take.

Best time to visit Belize, month by month

The right month depends on what you want. December and January bring the biggest crowds and the highest prices, but also the most reliable weather and the liveliest island scene. February and March offer those same great conditions at a slightly calmer pace, which makes them a sweet spot for many travelers. April is excellent too, though Easter week spikes in popularity, so plan around it.

May is a quiet shoulder month with strong weather and lower rates, ideal if you want value without much rain. June starts the green season, bringing afternoon showers, lush jungle and noticeably better prices. July and August stay warm and busy with summer families, and August is often the cheapest month of the year. September and October are the wettest and carry the highest hurricane risk, so they suit flexible, budget-focused travelers who lean on inland trips. November dries out again and offers great value before the December rush returns.

One more thing worth timing: whale shark season at Gladden Spit runs roughly from March through June, around the full moon. If swimming beside the ocean's biggest fish is on your list, plan your trip to match. Otherwise, almost any month works, since there's truly no bad time to visit Belize.

Free and budget-friendly things to do

You don't need a big budget to enjoy Belize. Plenty of the best experiences cost little or nothing. Swimming at The Split on Caye Caulker is free, and so is wandering its sandy streets at sunset. Many beaches are open and easy to reach, and a day of sunbathing and swimming costs only what you spend on a fruit smoothie. Walking the colorful streets of San Ignacio or exploring a local market gives you culture for free.

To stretch your money further, ride the local buses instead of private transfers, and eat where locals eat. A plate of rice and beans or a stack of fry jacks costs just a few dollars and tastes fantastic. Book guides directly when you can, and travel in green season for lower rates across hotels and tours. With a little planning, Belize becomes far more affordable than its reputation suggests, and you still get the reef, the ruins and the jungle that make it special.

What to pack for Belize

Packing for Belize is easy once you know the rhythm of the trip. You'll move between hot jungle and bright beaches, so light, quick-dry clothing wins every time. Bring a swimsuit or two, since you'll be in and out of the water constantly. Water shoes protect your feet on rocky reef entries and slippery cave floors, and they're worth the space in your bag. A light rain jacket helps in green season, when afternoon showers roll through fast and clear just as quickly.

For sun and bugs, pack reef-safe sunscreen and a good insect repellent, because the tropical sun and jungle mosquitoes are both no joke. A dry bag keeps your phone and camera safe on boat days and cave trips. Don't forget a reusable water bottle, a hat, and polarized sunglasses for the glare off the water. Finally, carry some small US or Belize bills for buses, tips and roadside food, since not every village takes cards. Pack smart, travel light, and you'll spend less time fussing and more time exploring.

A country that's easy to love

What stays with people after a Belize trip is rarely just one big attraction. It's the feeling of the place. It's the guide who shares a childhood story while you climb a temple. It's the howler monkeys waking you at dawn, the smell of grilled lobster on the beach, and the slow grin you get when you finally stop checking the clock. Belize makes that kind of unwinding feel natural, almost inevitable.

Few destinations pack this much variety into such a friendly, easygoing package. Whether you have five days or fifteen, Belize gives you a trip that's big on adventure and refreshingly low on stress. Belize travel tips that actually help

The little things that make a Belize trip smoother, safer and cheaper. Learned from real travelers, not brochures.

📅When to visit Belize

The dry season runs from late November through May. Skies stay clear, seas stay calm, and reef trips run on schedule. That's why it's also the busiest and priciest window, peaking around Easter. If you want the same weather for less, aim for April or early May.

The green season, June through November, brings afternoon showers and lush jungle. Prices drop, sometimes by 20 to 40 percent, and August is often the cheapest month of all. Hurricane risk peaks in September and October, so build in flexibility and lean on inland trips if you travel then.

💵Money and budgeting

Belize is not the cheapest country in Central America, but it's easy to manage. The Belize dollar is locked at two to one with the US dollar, so the math is simple. Carry small bills for buses, fruit stands and roadside food. Cards work in towns and resorts, but cash rules in villages.

To save, eat where locals eat. Fry jacks for breakfast and rice and beans for lunch cost a few dollars. Book local guides directly when you can. Public buses are cheap and full of local life, though domestic flights save serious time between the islands and the south.

🛟Staying safe and being a good guest

Tourist areas like San Pedro, Caye Caulker, Placencia and San Ignacio are generally safe. Use normal travel sense, keep valuables secure, and book with reputable operators. Belize City has rougher pockets, so most visitors pass through quickly on the way to the islands or jungle.

Respect the reef and the wildlife. Wear reef-safe sunscreen, never touch coral, and don't feed the animals. Support family-run lodges and local guides. Belize protects about half its land and sea, and travelers who tread lightly help keep it that way.

🎒What to pack and book ahead

Pack light, quick-dry clothes, water shoes, bug spray and reef-safe sunscreen. Bring a dry bag for boat days. Popular tours like the ATM Cave, the Great Blue Hole and whale shark trips sell out, so reserve those a few days ahead in busy season. Most island snorkeling trips you can grab the day before.
